📊 SRE Latency Metrics: Why Percentiles Matter

Understanding Distribution vs Averages in Real-World Latency

🎯 The Problem with Averages

Look at the distribution above. Even when 95% of users have great experience (<200ms), a few slow requests can make the average misleading. The 95th percentile tells you what your worst 5% of users actually experience.

📊 Normal Distribution vs Reality

Toggle the "Show Normal Distribution" to see the difference!

In statistics theory, if latency followed a normal (Gaussian) distribution:

  • 68% of requests would be within ±1 standard deviation (μ ± 1σ)
  • 95% of requests would be within ±2 standard deviations (μ ± 2σ)
  • 99.7% of requests would be within ±3 standard deviations (μ ± 3σ)

But real latency distributions are NOT normal! They have long tails, outliers, and often multiple peaks. This is why we need percentiles, not just standard deviations.

❌ Why This Query is Insufficient

avg(http_request_latency_seconds{job='ecommerce-api'})

Problems:

  • Hides outliers that affect real users
  • Can show "good" average while users suffer
  • Not suitable for SLO definitions
  • Doesn't reveal long-tail distribution

✅ Why Histogram Percentiles Are Superior

histogram_quantile(0.95, sum(rate(http_request_latency_seconds_bucket{job='ecommerce-api'}[1m])) by (le, endpoint) )

Advantages:

  • User-focused: Shows experience of worst 5% of users
  • SLO-ready: Perfect for "95% of requests < 500ms" targets
  • Aggregatable: Works across multiple service instances
  • Efficient: Pre-aggregated buckets, fast queries
  • Alertable: Reliable threshold for meaningful alerts

🔬 Statistical Deep Dive

Why 95th percentile ≈ μ + 2σ in normal distributions:

In a perfect normal distribution, 95% of values fall within 2 standard deviations of the mean. However, real latency data is almost never normally distributed:

  • Right-skewed: Most requests are fast, but some are very slow (long tail)
  • Bimodal: Cache hits vs cache misses create two distinct populations
  • Bounded: Latency can't be negative, unlike normal distributions

This is exactly why percentiles are more reliable than standard deviations for SRE work. Toggle between scenarios to see how the actual distribution compares to the theoretical normal curve!
